How to Study the Bible

A Reference Guide · Based on the Method of Kay Arthur

The Inductive Method

Kay Arthur's approach, developed through Precept Ministries, is called Inductive Bible Study — letting the text speak for itself rather than reading into it. The method moves in three stages that build on each other.

"The Bible is God's love letter to you. It is life itself. Handle it with care — and with diligence."
— Kay Arthur
1
Observation — What does it say?

Read the text carefully and repeatedly. Ask the 5 W's and an H. Note every detail without yet drawing conclusions.

2
Interpretation — What does it mean?

Let scripture interpret scripture. Study context, grammar, and genre. Ask what the author intended for the original audience.

3
Application — What does it mean for me?

Only after understanding the meaning: identify principles and apply them specifically to your life today.

Key principle: Application without proper observation and interpretation leads to misuse of scripture. The sequence is not optional — meaning must be established before application is valid.
Why "Inductive"?

Inductive reasoning moves from specific observations to general conclusions — the opposite of deductive. You study what the text actually says, then draw conclusions from it. This guards against reading your assumptions into scripture (called eisegesis) and instead draws meaning out of it (exegesis).

Observation

The foundation of good Bible study. Read the passage slowly, repeatedly — at least three times. The goal is to see everything the author wrote, nothing more, nothing less.

Ask the 5 W's + H

Who?Who is speaking? Who are the people involved? Who is the audience?
What?What is happening? What are the key events, ideas, or commands?
When?When does this take place? Are there time references or sequences?
Where?Where is this happening? What geographical context matters?
Why?Why is this being said or done? Are reasons or purposes stated?
How?How is something done or accomplished? What method or means?

Things to look for in the text

→
Key words

Words that are repeated, emphasized, or essential to understanding the passage. Mark them consistently throughout (see Marking tab).

→
Lists

When an author lists things — qualities, commands, reasons — number them. Lists often reveal the structure of an argument.

→
Contrasts & comparisons

Look for "but," "however," "likewise," "just as." These signal comparison (A is like B) or contrast (A but not B).

→
Terms of conclusion

Words like "therefore," "so," "for this reason" signal that what follows is grounded in what came before. Always ask what the "therefore" is there for.

→
Expressions of time

"After this," "in those days," "until." Time markers reveal sequence and causation.

→
Terms of purpose

"In order that," "so that," "lest" — these reveal the why behind an action or command.

"Don't rush observation. Most poor Bible interpretation comes not from wrong thinking — but from too little looking."

Interpretation

After thorough observation, ask: what did this mean to the original author and audience? The primary meaning is singular and historical — a passage cannot mean something today that it didn't mean when written.

1
Let scripture interpret scripture

The best commentary on the Bible is the Bible itself. When a passage is unclear, find other places where the same truth is addressed more plainly. Never build a doctrine on a single obscure verse.

2
Consider the context — near and far

Immediate context: the verses immediately before and after. Chapter context: the argument of the whole chapter. Book context: the author's purpose in writing. Historical context: what was happening in the world at the time?

3
Understand the genre

Poetry uses metaphor and hyperbole. Prophecy includes symbolic language. Epistles make logical arguments. History records events. Each genre requires appropriate reading (see Genres tab).

4
Study key word meanings

Use a concordance to find the original Hebrew or Greek word. Many English words translate multiple originals — knowing the difference often unlocks the meaning. A Bible dictionary or Strong's Concordance helps here.

5
Identify the main theme of the passage

After your study, write a one-sentence summary of what the passage is about. This becomes your "segment title" and helps you remember the flow of a book.

Context rule of thumb: Read at minimum the entire chapter — preferably the entire book — before drawing conclusions about a single verse. A text without a context is a pretext.

Application

The final — and most personal — step. Application is not merely intellectual agreement. It demands a response: a change in thinking, attitude, or action. Arthur stresses that knowledge of scripture that doesn't change life is dangerous (James 1:22).

The application bridge

Application moves from timeless principle → specific action. First identify the principle the passage teaches (valid for all people, all times). Then ask how that principle specifically applies to your situation today.

Application questions to ask yourself

→
Is there a sin to avoid or confess?

Does this passage reveal something in my life that is contrary to God's will?

→
Is there a promise to claim?

Is there a conditional or unconditional promise here? What are the conditions, if any?

→
Is there a command to obey?

Is there a clear directive for believers? Is this addressed to me specifically?

→
Is there an example to follow or avoid?

Do the characters in the passage model something worth imitating — or warning against?

→
Is there a truth about God to believe?

What does this passage reveal about God's character, ways, or purposes?

"The greatest Bible scholar is not the one who knows the most — but the one who obeys the most."

Marking Your Bible

Arthur teaches a system of marking key words and themes with consistent symbols and colors. This transforms reading into active study and reveals patterns across a book. Use colored pencils, not highlighters (which bleed).

How to do it: Choose a key word, decide on a symbol and/or color, and mark it the same way every time it appears — even across different books of the Bible. Consistency is the whole point.

Building a chapter theme chart

After finishing each chapter, write a one-sentence summary of its main theme. These accumulate into a book outline that helps you remember the whole.

Example — Romans
Ch. 1Universal sinfulness of humanity — Gentiles under condemnation Ch. 2Jews also under judgment — the law doesn't save Ch. 3Righteousness from God through faith in Christ Ch. 4Abraham justified by faith, not works — the pattern Ch. 5Peace with God, and Adam vs. Christ — the two heads …and so on through ch. 16

Reading by Genre

The Bible contains many types of literature, each with its own rules of reading. Ignoring genre is one of the most common sources of misinterpretation.

Narrative
~40% of the OT
Prophecy
Major & minor prophets
Poetry / Wisdom
Psalms, Proverbs, Job
Epistles
NT letters to churches
Apocalyptic
Daniel, Revelation
Gospel / Biography
Matthew, Mark, Luke, John
Narrative (History)

Not every story is prescriptive — descriptive text records what happened, not necessarily what should happen. Characters' choices are not automatically models to follow. Ask: what does the author want the reader to learn from this account?

Poetry & Wisdom

Hebrew poetry uses parallelism, not rhyme. Proverbs are general wisdom principles, not absolute promises. Psalms express emotion and theology through imagery — not every metaphor is literal. Don't over-literalize poetic language.

Epistles (Letters)

NT letters follow a logical argument — read the whole letter to understand any part. Identify the occasion (why was it written?) and the audience. Commands addressed to first-century churches require discernment about universal vs. culturally specific application.

Prophecy

Distinguish between predictive and forth-telling prophecy. Some prophecy has already been fulfilled; some is future. OT prophecy often has a near fulfillment (for Israel) and a far fulfillment (messianic/eschatological). Don't flatten the two.

Apocalyptic

Revelation and Daniel use rich symbolic language — numbers, beasts, colors — that had recognizable meaning to original audiences. Understand the symbols in their historical context before assigning contemporary meaning. Humility is essential.

Law (Torah)

Three categories: moral (universally binding — the Ten Commandments), ceremonial (fulfilled in Christ), and civil (specific to Israel's theocracy). Understanding which category a law falls into is essential before applying it today.

Tools & Habits

Arthur recommends a modest toolkit. The goal is to study the text itself — reference tools support but never replace careful reading of the Bible.

Essential reference tools

1
A good study Bible

Choose one with cross-references, book introductions, and maps. Arthur's New Inductive Study Bible is designed specifically for this method, with wide margins for notes.

2
Concordance

A complete concordance (e.g. Strong's) lets you find every occurrence of a word and look up the original Hebrew or Greek. Essential for key word study.

3
Bible dictionary

Provides historical, cultural, and geographical background. Helpful for understanding unfamiliar people, places, and customs.

4
Bible atlas

Geography profoundly shapes meaning in scripture. Knowing where events happened — terrain, proximity, significance — adds depth to observation.

Study habits Arthur recommends

DailyConsistent Study at the same time each day — it builds habit and signals to yourself that this is priority.
Pray first Ask for understanding before you open the text. Acknowledge dependence on the Holy Spirit as the author of scripture.
Write it down Keep a study notebook. Writing forces clarity of thought. Note observations, questions, and what you're learning.
Read the whole book first Before studying verse by verse, read the entire book in one sitting to grasp the author's overall flow and purpose.
Memorize key verses Scripture memory makes the word available in your mind without the book. Even a few verses per month accumulates significantly over years.
Study with others Arthur strongly encourages small group inductive study — others see what you miss, and accountability deepens application.
"Handle accurately the word of truth." — 2 Timothy 2:15. The goal of all Bible study is not information, but transformation.
