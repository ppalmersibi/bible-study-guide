Biblical Interpretation

A systematic guide to Sacred Hermeneutics — the science and art of determining what the Bible means and how it applies to life today.

What Is Hermeneutics?

Sacred Hermeneutics is the study of methods for determining the meaning of biblical text. The core thesis: the Bible can be understood — and understood alike — by all people who approach it rightly.

"No book, including the Bible, is written without an intended meaning in the mind of the author. God wrote a book to communicate certain ideas in His mind and will — and man can come to know them."

Why interpretation matters

The problem
Post-modern relativism

The view that "my interpretation is as good as yours" — driven by existentialism and the rejection of absolutes — has invaded the church. This position is both philosophically incoherent and spiritually dangerous.

The answer
Objective meaning exists

God wrote scripture to communicate. He expects people to understand it — and to understand it alike. The goal of hermeneutics is to recover the author's intended meaning, not to generate personal meanings.

Three theories this book refutes

1
Illumination

The idea that only the Holy Spirit directly enlightens each reader, making methodology unnecessary. This contradicts Paul's own instruction to "rightly handle the word of truth."

2
Existentialism

"Whatever it means to me is valid." This leads to as many interpretations as there are readers — which destroys any concept of authoritative scripture.

3
Despair

The view that since people disagree on scripture, agreement is impossible. But disagreement proves only that some are wrong — not that understanding is unachievable.

Why people misunderstand the Bible

Ignorance People don't read the text thoroughly enough — they rely on preachers and teachers instead of personal study.
Prejudice Pre-formed opinions and theological assumptions are read into the text rather than drawn from it.
Wrong method Using allegorical, mystical, or purely subjective approaches that bypass the author's plain intent.

The Proper Attitude

Chapter II argues that interpretation is not merely intellectual — it requires a spiritual posture. Wrong attitudes corrupt the process before it begins.

"The greatest priority of a Christian's life is coming to know God. And the primary means of knowing God is His Word."

Six essential attitudes

1
Hunger for God — not just information

Jesus rebuked the Pharisees who diligently studied scripture yet rejected Him (John 5:39–40). The goal of Bible study is knowing the Father, not accumulating knowledge.

2
Expectancy

"What will He tell me today?" Approach the text expecting to hear from God — not merely to complete a task or confirm existing beliefs.

3
Reverent awe

A healthy fear of God keeps the interpreter humble. "My flesh trembles in fear of you" (Ps. 119:120). This guards against treating scripture casually or manipulatively.

4
Humility

Willingness to be corrected. Bringing pre-formed conclusions to the text (prejudice) is one of the three chief reasons people misread the Bible.

5
Diligence

Serious study takes time. The Bible is not a book to be skimmed. Psalm 119 returns repeatedly to the theme of meditating on God's word day and night.

6
Obedience-readiness

The student must be willing to obey what is found. An interpreter who has already decided what they will and won't accept is not reading the text — they are using it.

Studying the Bible to win arguments, confirm a tradition, or justify a decision already made is a misuse of scripture — and produces distorted interpretation regardless of method.

Basic Assumptions

Four foundational assumptions that all sound biblical interpretation must accept:

Assumption 1
The Bible is divinely inspired

Scripture originated from God, not merely from human genius. This means it has unified, coherent meaning — and that meaning is authoritative.

Assumption 2
Jesus' words are God's words

The words Jesus spoke on earth — recorded in the four Gospels — are as authoritative as any other part of Scripture. He is the living Word made flesh.

Assumption 3
Our manuscripts are reliable

Through careful canonicity review and faithful transmission, we have received the Scripture in substantially accurate form. Jewish copyists and NT manuscript traditions both support this.

Assumption 4
Good translations are available

While all translations have weaknesses, we have access to faithful English translations. The NAS is recommended for word-for-word accuracy. Use multiple translations to check against each other.

On translations: word-for-word translations (like NAS/NASB) are preferred over dynamic equivalents for serious study. Dynamic equivalents (NIV, NLT) are more readable but may obscure exact language. Use both: read dynamic equivalents for flow, check word-for-word for precision.

Successive Readings

The core methodology: reading the same text repeatedly, each time with a different "angle" or focus.

"Read the text through multiple times before consulting any outside sources. Your first impressions from the text itself are irreplaceable."

The successive reading process

1
Read the entire book first
Before studying any passage in detail, read the whole biblical book in one sitting. Get the big picture — the author's purpose, tone, and overall argument. Don't read for detail; read for impression.
2
Read for the historical occasion
Who wrote this? To whom? When? What was happening in their world? Look for clues embedded in the text itself about the author's situation and the recipients' circumstances.
3
Read for the purpose
Why was this written? What problem is the author addressing? What response does he want from his readers? Many books state their purpose explicitly (cf. John 20:31; Luke 1:1–4).
4
Read for special emphases / key themes
Note words, phrases, or themes that recur. Repetition signals importance. What does the author keep returning to? These become the theological heartbeat of the book.
5
Read for audience weakness/need
What does the text imply about the readers' situation? Are they struggling with false teaching? Immaturity? Persecution? Practical sin? The author's exhortations reveal the audience's condition.
6
Only then: consult secondary literature
After your own successive readings, check commentaries, Bible dictionaries, and background sources. These supplement — they must never replace — your firsthand reading of the text.

Exegesis: getting closer

Thought diagrams (sentence analysis)

Break the text into units of thought — not grammatical diagrams, but thought structures. Write out the passage in your own words, grouping ideas by their logical relationships (main assertion, support, qualification, conclusion). This "syntax" work makes the argument of a passage visible.

Writing chapter / segment titles

After studying each chapter, write a one-sentence theme summary. These accumulate into a structural overview of the whole book — the closest thing to an outline the author left you.

Fundamental Rules

Chapter VII presents the core interpretive rules that govern reading any biblical passage. These rules govern all legitimate interpretation regardless of genre.

1
One intended meaning

Every passage has one meaning — the one the author intended. A text may have many applications, but it has only one meaning. Multiple contradictory interpretations cannot all be correct.

2
The simplest meaning is usually right

Scripture was written by farmers, shepherds, and fishermen — to people like themselves. God was not hiding truth in obscure language. If the plain meaning makes good sense, seek no other sense. Reject allegorical readings that ignore the obvious.

3
Let scripture interpret scripture

Unclear passages are best illuminated by clearer ones. No doctrine should rest on a single difficult verse. If your interpretation of one passage contradicts what the Bible teaches elsewhere, reconsider your interpretation.

4
Context is decisive

A text without its context is a pretext. Understand the immediate context (surrounding verses), chapter context, book context, and historical context before drawing conclusions.

5
Original audience first

Ask what the text meant to the original readers before asking what it means to you. The gap between original meaning and contemporary application must be crossed deliberately — not collapsed.

6
Grammar and word meaning matter

The original language (Hebrew/Greek) is the authoritative text. When precision matters, use a concordance to check the actual word used and its range of meaning in that era.

The allegorical method — reading hidden spiritual meanings behind historical events — has been popular across history but violates Rule 1 (one intended meaning) and Rule 2 (simplest is best). Speer rejects it as a legitimate method.

Tools of the Trade

Always do your own successive readings first. Tools exist to help you go deeper — not to tell you what to think before you've thought.

What Commands Are Binding?

Chapter IX addresses one of the most debated hermeneutical questions: which New Testament commands apply to Christians today — and how do we determine that?

The framework

The NT commands were spoken/written almost 2,000 years ago. But Jesus said "If you love me, you will obey what I command" (John 14:15) — without a time limit. The apostles wrote with the expectation that their letters would be read, understood, and obeyed (Ephesians 3:2–6; 2 Thessalonians 2:14–15).

Three questions to ask any command

1
Is it addressed to Christians universally?

Commands addressed to "the church," "brothers," or "all who call on the name of the Lord" are broadly binding. Commands to specific individuals in unique situations may not be.

2
Does it rest on a timeless principle?

If the reason behind the command is culturally bound, the command's application may be culturally limited — but the underlying principle remains. Separate the principle from its cultural expression.

3
Is it consistent with the whole NT?

A command understood as universally binding should be consistent with, not contradictory to, the broader teaching of the New Testament.

Jesus himself applied OT commands to His own day that were spoken centuries earlier (Matthew 15:4). The age of a command does not automatically limit its application.

Biblical Examples as Precedents

Chapter X asks: when do actions described in scripture become binding practices — not just illustrations?

When is an example binding?

1
Is it apostolically approved?

Examples explicitly endorsed by apostolic teaching or practice carry authority. The Lord's Supper on the first day of the week (Acts 20:7) is an approved example — binding on the church today.

2
Is it incidental or essential?

Paul sailed to preach — sailing is incidental. The church evangelized as they scattered — evangelism is essential. Ask whether the example fulfills a universal command or is just the method used in one circumstance.

3
Distinguish principle from cultural form

The "holy kiss" greeting (Romans 16:16) expressed the principle of showing brotherly affection — the principle is binding, the cultural form is not. Hugs, handshakes, and words of warmth fulfill the same principle today.

4
Is it universally practiced in the NT?

A practice that appears across multiple books, churches, and contexts carries stronger weight than a single occurrence. Consistent NT practice suggests universal intention.

Examples identified as binding today: weekly observance of the Lord's Supper (Acts 20:7), world evangelism (Acts 1–8), church discipline of the unrepentant (1 Cor. 5).

The Silence of Scripture

Chapter XI addresses a uniquely contested question: when the Bible doesn't mention something, does that silence permit it or forbid it?

"Do not add to what I command you and do not subtract from it." — Deuteronomy 4:2

Two ways silence functions

When silence permits
Generic commands allow variety

When God gives a general command without specifying the means, the silence on specifics permits freedom. "Go into all the world" doesn't specify transportation — any means may be used. Having a church building, using a sound system, choosing song books — these fall under the silence that permits.

When silence forbids
The Law of Inclusion

When God specifies the means, what is not included is excluded. God told Noah to build from gopher wood — no other wood was authorized. If a command specifies the category and God specifies within it, alternatives in that same category are excluded unless authorized elsewhere.

The authority pattern

The "authority pattern": required specifics commanded → alternatives in that category are excluded. General commands without specification → freedom in the specifics. The key is identifying whether a command is generic or specific, and what category it governs.

This is one of the most debated areas in hermeneutics. Applying the Law of Inclusion too broadly restricts what God has not restricted; applying it too narrowly opens the door to additions God has not authorized. Careful contextual judgment is required in each case.

Differences in Interpretation

Sincere, diligent Christians still disagree. Here is a framework for understanding why — and how to respond.

Why sincere interpreters disagree

1
Incomplete study

Most disagreements stem not from bad intent but from insufficient work. Conclusion-drawing before thorough successive reading and context study produces premature interpretations.

2
Tradition over text

What we have been taught shapes what we see. Breaking free of inherited interpretations requires courage and a willingness to follow the text wherever it leads.

3
Different hermeneutical systems

People using fundamentally different methods will reach different conclusions even reading the same text. The solution is not to tolerate all methods, but to agree on the right method — historical-grammatical interpretation.

4
Unwillingness to obey

Sometimes the text is clear — but the interpreter is unwilling to accept the conclusion. This is not a hermeneutical problem; it's a spiritual one.

Responding to disagreement

Unity in the body of Christ is not achieved by tolerating all interpretations — it is achieved by all parties returning to the text and following the method faithfully. Jesus prayed "that all of them may be one" (John 17) — and Paul grounded that unity in shared submission to scripture, not in agreeing to disagree.

"The goal of hermeneutics is not winning a debate — it is arriving at what God actually said, so we can obey what God actually requires."
